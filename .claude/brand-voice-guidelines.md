## Executive Summary

SlashTable's voice is the voice of a sharp, opinionated developer who builds tools they actually want to use. We talk like engineers talk to each other — direct, specific, occasionally funny, never corporate. We'd rather have half our audience love us and half ignore us than have everyone think we're fine.

Our brand takes heavy inspiration from PostHog's approach: be weird, be honest, be useful. We're a database tool built by people who got tired of the status quo, and that frustration-turned-into-action energy should come through in everything we write. We don't explain what a "data management paradigm" is — we show you how to query your Postgres in two clicks.

## We Are / We Are Not

The foundational brand identity anchor. Voice is constant — it doesn't change by channel or audience.

| We Are                                                                   | We Are Not                                                                         |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Direct** — say it in one sentence, not three paragraphs                | **Blunt to the point of being rude** — directness with empathy, not dismissiveness |
| **Opinionated** — we have strong views on how database tools should work | **Dogmatic** — we hold opinions loosely and change them when we're wrong           |
| **Technically honest** — we say what the tool does and doesn't do        | **Marketing-speak merchants** — no "revolutionary AI-powered data solutions"       |
| **Playful** — we have personality, humor, and a bit of weird energy      | **Unprofessional** — humor serves clarity, it never replaces substance             |
| **Builder-minded** — we think like the engineers who use our tool        | **Gatekeeping** — expertise doesn't mean exclusion; we welcome all skill levels    |
| **Transparent** — we share what we're building, why, and what's broken   | **Performatively vulnerable** — honesty isn't a brand exercise, it's a default     |

### Voice Attributes Detail

#### Attribute 1: Direct
- **What it means**: Lead with the point. If you're explaining something, don't warm up for three paragraphs — start with the explanation and expand later.
- **How it shows up**: Short sentences. Active voice. Explanation first, context second. No throat-clearing intros like "In today's world of modern databases..."
- **What to avoid**: Being so terse that you lose warmth. Directness isn't coldness.
- **Evidence**: PostHog principle — "If you're explaining something, don't wait three paragraphs to do so."
- **Confidence**: High

#### Attribute 2: Opinionated
- **What it means**: We have a point of view on how database tools should work. We don't hedge with "it depends" or "it's complicated" — we tell you what we think and show you why.
- **How it shows up**: Taking stances in blog posts, docs, and feature copy. Recommending specific approaches rather than listing all possible options equally.
- **What to avoid**: Being preachy or closed-minded. Have an opinion, but don't dismiss alternatives without reason.
- **Evidence**: PostHog principle — "Don't hedge. Have an opinion and provide an example."
- **Confidence**: High

#### Attribute 3: Technically Honest
- **What it means**: We describe our product in terms of what it actually does, not what it aspires to be. If something is a beta feature, we say so. If there's a limitation, we name it.
- **How it shows up**: Comparison pages that honestly assess competitors. Changelogs that mention what broke. Docs that call out edge cases.
- **What to avoid**: Self-deprecation as a brand strategy. Honesty doesn't mean underselling.
- **Evidence**: PostHog includes changelogs in their comparison articles and openly discusses limitations.
- **Confidence**: High

#### Attribute 4: Playful
- **What it means**: We're a tool people use every day — it shouldn't feel like filing taxes. Personality makes us memorable and approachable.
- **How it shows up**: Clever microcopy in the UI, occasional humor in docs and blog posts, mascot/illustration personality, social media that feels human.
- **What to avoid**: Forced jokes, meme references that age badly, humor that obscures the actual information.
- **Evidence**: PostHog's hedgehog mascots, "unhinged" social media, meme-driven ads that outperform corporate ones.
- **Confidence**: High

#### Attribute 5: Builder-Minded
- **What it means**: We think like the people who use our tool. We're building SlashTable because we needed it. That shared context means we skip the basics and get to the good stuff.
- **How it shows up**: Technical writing that assumes competence. Examples that use real-world scenarios, not toy data. Feature descriptions that explain the "why" alongside the "how."
- **What to avoid**: Assuming everyone has the same context. Call out things that seem obvious — "as you gain mastery, some things become second nature, but remember they weren't always so obvious."
- **Evidence**: PostHog principle on remembering that expertise wasn't always obvious.
- **Confidence**: High

#### Attribute 6: Transparent
- **What it means**: We build in the open. Roadmap, decisions, trade-offs, failures — they're all visible. Transparency builds trust faster than any marketing campaign.
- **How it shows up**: Public changelogs, open discussions about architecture decisions, honest pricing, no "contact sales" gatekeeping.
- **What to avoid**: Oversharing for the sake of appearing transparent. Share what's useful, not what's performative.
- **Evidence**: PostHog's entire company handbook is public — compensation, strategy, hiring decisions, even failed experiments.
- **Confidence**: High

## Brand Personality

- **Archetype**: The Opinionated Colleague — technically sharp, tells it like it is, but always willing to help you debug at 11pm
- **If our brand were a person**: A senior engineer who writes clear PRs, has strong opinions about ORMs, makes dry jokes in Slack, and genuinely cares about developer experience. They'd never say "synergy" unironically.
- **Core values expressed in voice**: Craft, honesty, respect for the user's time, and a belief that developer tools should be delightful — not just functional

## Messaging Framework

### Primary Value Proposition
SlashTable is a desktop database client that gets out of your way and lets you work.

Variations:
- "The database tool for people who'd rather be building" (casual)
- "Connect, explore, and manage your databases — fast" (functional)
- "Your databases. One app. No friction." (minimal)

### Key Message Pillars

1. **Speed & Simplicity**
   - Core idea: SlashTable is fast because it's a native app, and simple because we obsess over UX.
   - When to use: Feature pages, onboarding, comparison content
   - Example phrasing: "Connect to Postgres in two clicks. No browser tabs. No Electron bloat."

2. **Developer-First Design**
   - Core idea: Built by product engineers, for product engineers. Every feature answers a real workflow need.
   - When to use: Product descriptions, blog posts, landing page
   - Example phrasing: "We built SlashTable because we got tired of switching between five tools to do one job."

3. **Desktop-Native Power**
   - Core idea: Native performance matters. A database tool should feel as responsive as your code editor.
   - When to use: Technical comparisons, performance discussions, architecture posts
   - Example phrasing: "Built with Tauri. Not another Electron wrapper pretending to be fast."

### Competitive Positioning
- vs. pgAdmin: "We respect what pgAdmin built. We just think database tools should've kept evolving."
- vs. DBeaver: "All the power, none of the Java startup time."
- vs. TablePlus: "Similar philosophy, different execution. We're open about our roadmap and opinionated about UX."
- vs. CLI: "We love the terminal too. SlashTable is for when you want to see your data, not just query it."

## Tone-by-Context Matrix

Voice is constant. Tone flexes by context. These three dimensions adjust:

| Context                   | Formality  | Energy      | Technical Depth | Key Principle                         |
| ------------------------- | ---------- | ----------- | --------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| Website / landing page    | Low-Medium | High        | Low-Medium      | Hook fast, show don't tell            |
| Documentation             | Low        | Medium      | High            | Be useful, not academic               |
| Blog posts                | Low        | Medium-High | Medium-High     | Have a take, back it up               |
| Social media              | Low        | High        | Low             | Personality over polish               |
| Changelog / release notes | Low        | Medium      | High            | Ship notes, not press releases        |
| Error messages (in-app)   | Low        | Calm        | Medium          | Help, don't blame                     |
| Onboarding UI             | Low        | Warm        | Low-Medium      | Guide without patronizing             |
| GitHub issues / community | Low        | Medium      | High            | Peer-to-peer, not support-to-customer |

### Context-Specific Guidelines

#### Website / Landing Page
- **Overall tone**: Confident and concise. Every sentence earns its place.
- **Opening approach**: Lead with what the user gets, not who we are.
- **Do's**: Use short, punchy copy. Show the product early. Be specific about what's different.
- **Don'ts**: Don't open with "Welcome to SlashTable." Don't use stock photography. Don't write anything that could've been written by any other SaaS company.
- **Example**: "Your Postgres, MySQL, and SQLite databases. One app. Stupid fast."

#### Documentation
- **Overall tone**: Friendly expert. Like pair-programming with someone who's done this before.
- **Opening approach**: Start with what the reader will accomplish, then walk through how.
- **Do's**: Use real examples with real data shapes. Link generously. Call out non-obvious gotchas.
- **Don'ts**: Don't write "In this tutorial, we will learn how to..." Just start doing the thing. Don't hide the answer behind three paragraphs of context.
- **Example**: "To connect to a remote Postgres instance, you'll need the host, port, and credentials. Here's how:" (then immediately show the steps)

#### Blog Posts
- **Overall tone**: Opinionated and personal. These read like the author actually cares.
- **Opening approach**: Start with a take, a question, or a frustration — not a definition.
- **Do's**: Take stances. Share what you learned. Be honest about trade-offs.
- **Don'ts**: Don't write "What is a database?" content. Don't hedge every opinion with "but it depends."
- **Example**: "Most database GUIs are designed for DBAs, not product engineers. That's a mistake."

#### Social Media
- **Overall tone**: Human, playful, slightly unhinged. This is where personality lives.
- **Do's**: Be funny when it's natural. Share work-in-progress. Engage with the community. Use memes if they're good.
- **Don'ts**: Don't sound like a brand account. Don't use hashtags unironically. Don't post "We're thrilled to announce..."
- **Example**: "just spent 3 hours debugging a query that had a missing semicolon. anyway here's a new feature"

## Terminology Guide

### Must-Use Terms
| Term       | Usage                                | Instead Of                          | Example                                           |
| ---------- | ------------------------------------ | ----------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| SlashTable | Always one word, capital S capital T | Slash Table, slashtable, Slashtable | "SlashTable connects to your database in seconds" |

### Preferred Terms
| Term        | Usage                                | Example                                       |
| ----------- | ------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------- |
| connect     | For database connections             | "Connect to your Postgres instance"           |
| explore     | For browsing schema/data             | "Explore your tables and relationships"       |
| query       | For running SQL                      | "Write and run queries with autocomplete"     |
| desktop app | When describing the product category | "SlashTable is a desktop app, not a web tool" |

### Avoid These Terms
| Term                    | Reason                                  | Alternative                       |
| ----------------------- | --------------------------------------- | --------------------------------- |
| solution                | Corporate jargon                        | "tool" or "app"                   |
| leverage                | Nobody talks like this                  | "use"                             |
| seamless                | Meaningless                             | describe what's actually smooth   |
| robust                  | Vague                                   | describe the specific capability  |
| enterprise-grade        | Alienating unless in enterprise context | describe the actual feature       |
| revolutionize / disrupt | Hyperbolic startup cliche               | just describe what it does        |
| utilize                 | Fancy "use"                             | "use"                             |
| empower                 | Corporate filler                        | say what the user can actually do |

### Never-Use Terms
| Term                  | Reason                                                                                |
| --------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| whitelist / blacklist | Use allowlist / denylist — clearer and more inclusive                                 |
| simple / easy         | Subjective — what's easy for you might not be for the reader                          |
| just                  | Minimizes effort — "just run the migration" implies it's trivial when it might not be |

## Language That Works

### Top Phrases
1. **"Your databases. One app."** — Context: tagline / hero, Why it works: immediately communicates the value prop in five words
2. **"Built because we needed it."** — Context: about page / origin story, Why it works: signals authenticity, aligns with builder-minded voice
3. **"No browser tabs. No Electron bloat. No waiting."** — Context: feature comparison, Why it works: rule-of-three negative framing highlights what's different

### Questions That Engage
1. **"Why does your database tool take longer to start than your database?"** — Leads to: discussion of native performance vs. Electron
2. **"What if your database GUI was as fast as your terminal?"** — Leads to: positioning SlashTable as the bridge between CLI speed and GUI usability

---

## Language to Avoid

### Anti-Patterns
1. **"We're excited to announce..."** — Problem: generic, every company says this. Better: just announce the thing. "SlashTable 0.2 is out. Here's what's new."
2. **"In today's fast-paced world of data..."** — Problem: throat-clearing filler. Better: skip to the point.
3. **"SlashTable is a next-generation database management platform"** — Problem: jargon soup. Better: "SlashTable is a desktop database client. It's fast."
4. **Hedging everything with "it depends"** — Problem: frustrating and adds no value. Better: have an opinion, then explain the nuances.

## Content Examples

### Excellent Example
> **Release note**: "v0.1.2 — Added MySQL support. Fixed a bug where large tables would freeze the grid (sorry about that). Keyboard shortcuts now work in the query editor. Full changelog on GitHub."
>
> *Why this works*: Direct, honest (acknowledges the bug), specific, includes a link. No corporate preamble. Reads like a human wrote it.

### Example to Avoid
> **Release note**: "We are thrilled to announce the release of SlashTable version 0.1.2, which represents a significant milestone in our journey to revolutionize the database management experience. This release includes several exciting new features and improvements designed to empower product engineers."
>
> *Why this doesn't work*: Could have been written by any company about any product. "Thrilled," "revolutionize," "empower" are empty calories. The reader still doesn't know what changed.
